Retail Food and Diabetes

Will our food consumption affect our health?

Our daily consumption of retail food can be seen kind of our personal healthy behavior. But how much will they affect our health in aspects of diabetes? What is the relationship between different preferences for beverages, meat and materials and diabetes? We try to figure this out in this experiment.

​​In this experiment, we choose to use the decision tree and random forest model to see the contribution of each feature since we have data for grocery consumption in 11 categories and these models are capable of fitting complex datasets. We also continued the linear regression model as comparison.

It is noticeable that the consumption of beverages is highly correlated with prevalence and has the highest correlation coefficient of 0.62. Sugar and sweeteners and fats and oils’ coefficients are also relatively high (0.6 and 0.48 respectively). The intuition is that people who have a higher consumption of beverages are more likely to develop diabetes, hence the higher diabetes prevalence rate.

Using Retail Sales Data to Predict Diabetes Prevalence --Random Forest Model

Random Forest Model We then developed a random forest model and randomized search over the hyperparameters, the default is 5 fold cross-fold validation. The parameters of the estimator used to apply these methods are optimized by cross-validated search over parameter settings. The result is shown as below:

We also measure a feature’s importance by looking at how much the tree nodes that use that feature reduce impurity on average (across all trees in the forest).

As the figure suggests, beverage volume sales per capita contributed the most to the diabetes prevalence rate in this model.

Test the Importance of different categories on diabetes --- Linear Regression

We also tested this dataset using linear regression. The result is summarized as follows:

The p-value of beverage sales per capita is very small, which suggests that it is statistically significant in providing information in predicting the region’s diabetes prevalence rate. The R-squared value is 0.979, which means the data is well fitted to the regression line, and 97.9% variability in prevalence can be explained by retail sales. We hence conclude that beverage retails sales can significantly affect the region’s diabetes prevalence rate. We therefore can reject the null hypothesis.
